# Architecture: why continuous-time, first-order, return-to-zero

**The chip is one modulator producing one bitstream that is decimated two
ways at once** by the companion FPGA (a PolarFire SoC, which also supplies
a clean low-jitter clock):

| Path | Bandwidth | Target | Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fast | ~1 MHz (OSR 25) | ≥ 6–7 ENOB | protection / trip decisions |
| Precision | ~100 kHz (OSR 250) | 10–12 ENOB | measurement |

Every choice below is logged with its rationale (and its *reopen
conditions*) in the repository's append-only
[decision log](https://gitlab.com/pthomas1/sigma-delta/-/blob/main/DESIGN.md);
this chapter is the narrative version.

**Continuous-time, active-RC.** A CT loop's input network is a resistor
into a virtual ground — intrinsically anti-aliasing, no switched-capacitor
front end, no boosted clocks. The loop coefficient k = T<sub>s</sub>/(R·C)
lands at friendly on-chip values (~40 kΩ high-res poly, 1–2 pF MiM cap) at
f<sub>s</sub> = 50 MHz. The costs — sensitivity to the feedback pulse
*shape* and to clock jitter — are exactly the things the architecture then
has to manage, which is what the rest of this page is about.

**First order, 1 bit.** One integrator, one comparator, both reused
unchanged by a future second-order upgrade. The performance budget
(measured, not just the textbook formula): the linear model promises
74.5 dB SQNR at OSR 250, but a first-order loop's in-band error is
**pattern tones, not white noise** — long-run tier-0 simulation converges
to ~66 dB SNDR, and 16k-bit windows scatter over 54–67 dB depending on
dither seed. That measured gap is the strongest argument for the
second-order upgrade if the 12-bit target firms up; it is also why the CI
assertions use loose SNDR floors rather than exact values (a chaotic
modulator's per-window SNDR is not a stable regression quantity).

**Return-to-zero (RZ) feedback DAC — chosen by experiment.** A
single-ended NRZ DAC's dominant error is inter-symbol interference: the
energy a feedback pulse delivers depends on the *previous* bit (rise and
fall edges don't cancel), which demodulates shaped noise back into the
band. An RZ pulse pays its edges every cycle, making the per-bit charge
history-independent — at the price of doubled jitter sensitivity (retired
here by the external clock) and higher peak currents. The four-corner
simulation matrix ({NRZ, RZ} × {edge asymmetry, jitter}) confirmed the
presumption; the comparison report is generated by `make report`
({{report_links}}).

**Excess loop delay is modeled from day one.** The loop budget allocates a
full clock cycle for comparator regeneration + retiming DFF + DAC
settling; at 50 MHz that's a first-order effect, not a refinement. The
comparator gets a hard spec from this: full regeneration within its 10 ns
half-period, because a *soft* decision leaks through the DAC as unshaped
noise (a ~25 dB SNDR penalty was measured when violated).

**Area reality check.** A TinyTapeout 1×2 analog tile is 160×225 µm. The
budget puts the full first-order chip at ~18,000 µm² ≈ 50% of one 1×2
tile pair — comfortable; the second-order upgrade would push ~75%, arguing
for a 2×2 purchase if that's the ambition.
